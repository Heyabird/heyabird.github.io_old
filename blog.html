Why I started coding

04.02.20

Heya, World! This is my first blog post in my first and newly birthed self-made website. In my first post, I wanted to tell you a bit about how I started my coding journey.


From philosophy and art to code...

During college and 2.5 years after college, I immersed myself in philosophy, art, and music. As you might know, thse are not the most "practical" fields in terms of generating a sustainable income, but I am happy that I "naively" explored those passions before I tapped into something more "practical". This is because, during the last 2.5 years living as a deep thinker and artist, I could start cultivating a vision for myself.

First, by studying philosophy and art at Wesleyan University in Connecticut, I learned alot about the world. My major was the infamously rigorous College of Social Studies (a writing- and reading-heavy major that combines philosophy, history, economics, and politics -- also seen as the 'pre-law' major). I also took a significant amount of art and art history courses, graduating 1 credit short of also being a Studio Art/Sculpture major. Outside of classrooms, I acquired interesting experiences (TAing at a maximum-security prison, interpreting for North Korean refugees, making a graphic novel after interviewing factory workers in 13 Chinese cities, starting a community magazine, etc.). This is when I really trained my critical thinking skills, questioned the purpose of my life, and identified some of the problems in our society I want to solve.

When I moved to Los Angeles after college, I found myself working as a full-stack artist, doing various creative contract works (designing posters & logos, curating shows, ghost-producing music, singing, DJing, content writing, etc.). I also worked in a design agency, a cafe, a grocery store, an event-planning organization, a music school, and a startup. Doing so, I started to expand my creative muscle and imagined out-of-the-box solutions to the social problems I learned about during college and post-college.

With so many questions in my head and problems I want to solve, I felt like the natural next step for me was to learn coding, because (1), it is one of the most practical tools solve real life problems, (2), it can offer a sustainable means of living, and (3), the process of coding felt very creative and fun when I tried to dabble in it on my own. And, to be very honest, I was starting to feel depressed in my life in LA, where I felt like I was constantly worried about money while not challenging myself to live my full potential. Because I have a background in graphic design, I started off taking a full-time online UX design course via Bloc, only to realize I liked their sub-course on coding more than the main UX course. I also missed having a supportive learning community, but I didn't want to go through the bureacratic hoops nor pay an overly expensive tuition that traditional schools require. So, I started to look at bootcamps.


LEARNing how to code @ LEARN, San Diego

This is when I decided to move to San Diego and attend a coding bootcamp called LEARN Academy. I did a lot of rigorous research and weighed my options in what bootcamp I could attend. I looked at the giant corporate bootcamps in Los Angeles and San Francisco, but LEARN in San Diego totally won my heart. This was not only because my brother lived in San Diego and San Diego has better beaches than the other two cities; when I read student testimonals on LEARN's website and listened to LEARN C.E.O. Chelsea Kaufman talk with community members on LEARN's Collaboration Code Radio, I instantly knew this was the place for me. I could tell that LEARN students and staffs really cared about fostering a family in their collaborative learning environment. I also appreciated that LEARN has heavy women leadership involvement compared to other coding bootcamps. So, I sold my Tesla stocks, took out a loan, applied for their women in tech scholarship, and committed.

I plan to write more about my LEARNing journey on a separate blogpost, but I really wanted to give a shout-out to LEARN here because it has been exactly the place I need to be at. Perhaps because it is a small local startup, or because it focuses so much on collaboration, LEARN carries a strange sense of warmth to it that other corporate bootcamps lack. I think I was lucky too, as the 18 other students in my current cohort (named Alpha 2020) happen to be terribly kind, authentic, talented, and funny (seriously, there are so many moments in class I cannot breathe because I am laughing so hard). The instructors and staffs are also so genuine, supportive, and GOOD at their job -- it really made my transition into the coding world so exciting.

Two months into LEARNing, I have already been building full-stack projects that aim to tackle real life issues. It would be a lie if I said this has been an easy journey (I am completely out of my comfort zone and my neck, brain, and eyeballs hurt from so much learning), but deciding to learn how to code, especially through LEARN, has been one of the best choices I have made in my life. I am excited to see how my previous soft and hard skills can combine with my new tech skills to produce creative and practical projects. To be continued...!
