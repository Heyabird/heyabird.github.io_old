A. USER SURVEY:

Link to Survey: Click here

I kicked off this journey of UX/UI designing for this app by conducting user survey. I used different social media groups to find strangers (and friends) who would help me out by answering some questions about their usage (or non-usage) of public transportation apps.


Examples of Survey Charts:



Survey Findings:

- Public transportation apps are in demand. 93.3% of the responders who use public transportation use apps to navigate themselves.

- 93.3% of the responders who use public transportation use it to save money. It seems important to show the transportation fee on public transportation apps.

- The most popular answer for ‘what features could be improved on the app you use’ were: transportation time accuracy and user interface. Both of these features can be focused on the app we produce.

- One interesting response on why a responder does not use apps for public transportation was how online maps are hard to read. One possible way to target older generations is to make apps that can easily change font sizes.

- Google Maps seems to be the most popular public transportation app used, followed by Citymapper.





B. COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS:

Comparing Google Maps and City Mapper

Because Google Maps and Citymapper were the two most popular maps from my survey, I decided to compare the two. After market research, I found out that both Google Maps and Citymapper are widely-used and highly competent app options to use for public transportation as one navigates the city of Chicago. Google Maps’ strength lies in its large scale and universality while Citymapper excels in its focus on public transportation and approachable and friendly design.

E. USER TESTING:

I asked three different users to perform one task: to find out which buses arrive at what time at Bus Stop A, which is a stop at the intersection of Washington and State.

All three participants successfully performed the task, although each user experience was slightly different. There was one participant (second participant) who felt a bit lost in the first screen. He told me that he would have an easier time navigating if there were colored graphics to help him understand what is going on. The first and third participant smoothly breezed through the process. Overall, there was no major problem in the MVP prototype, and I am eager to proceed in further developing the prototype with colors and detailed graphics.

F. UI DESIGN (Bus List Screens):


Using the findings from user testing, I made a UI design for bust listing screen. Because I want to make this app for android, I used Material Design Guidleines. I iterated two versions of it using feedback and referencing the guideline.



What changed from version 1 (left) to 2 (right):

a. List information architecture reorganized using Material Design Guidelines: Anatomy.

b. Font colors reselected according to Material Design Guidelines.

c. Background color reselected to a suggested color in Material Design (#E0F2F1). The color scheme ratio from foreground to background color is 9.68:1 now.
